# Investment Principles

The philosophy we fall back to when making a decision and doing final checks. These govern
*how we find and hold ideas* — they sit above any individual thesis. This file will grow and
change; see the changelog at the bottom.

> **How Claude uses this:** before finalizing any thesis verdict or positioning note, run the
> idea through the **Pre-Investment Gate** below and surface any principle it fails. A thesis
> that fails a gate isn't necessarily dead — but the failure must be named and justified.

## The one-line strategy
**Harvest behavioral & structural mispricing with patient, diversified capital — and let it compound.**
The five principles below are one reinforcing chain, not a checklist of separate rules:
efficiency (P1) → the only edge is behavioral/structural (P2) → which takes time to realize, so
go long-term (P3) → with a portfolio that survives being early (P4) → all to keep capital
compounding undisturbed (P5), which is the actual goal.

## Pre-Investment Gate (the checklist Claude runs)
A thesis should be able to answer all four before it informs a real decision:

1. **Priced-in test (P1):** If this is obvious with public data + AI, *what is the reason it
   isn't already in the price?* If the only answer is "I'm smarter/earlier" → **reject.**
2. **Edge test (P2):** Name the **irrationality or forced flow**. **Who is on the other side,
   and why are they irrational or forced?** What corrects it, and roughly when?
3. **Horizon test (P3):** Does the payoff hold on a **1-year-plus** view *without* needing
   precise timing?
4. **Portfolio-fit test (P4):** Sized so failure is a **dent, not a wipeout**? Does it add
   diversification, or pile into a crowded trade?
5. **Compounding test (P5):** Does this let capital **compound undisturbed** — a durable multi-year
   runway, no risk of ruin, minimal churn/tax/fee friction?

---

## P1 — Assume it's already priced in (the efficiency prior)
**Principle.** The market is fast and everyone has AI. Default assumption: all public information
is already in the price. An *informational* edge — "I found a fact or insight others missed" — is
almost certainly an illusion.

**Why.** Overestimating your informational edge is the most common way to lose money. Humility
about efficiency is the correct starting point.

**How to apply.** This is a *prior*, not an absolute (taken literally it would mean never invest).
Its job is to put the **burden of proof on the idea**: the default is "priced in," and you may only
override it by naming a specific behavioral/structural reason (→ P2).

## P2 — The edge is irrationality, not discovery
**Principle.** Seek opportunities created by **market irrationality rooted in human behavior**
(fear, greed, herding, recency bias, narrative overshoot) and by **structural forces**
(forced/non-economic sellers, passive-index flows, mandates) — *not* by believing you've thought of
something no one else has.

**Why.** Information is arbitraged away in seconds; behavior and structure are not, because they're
driven by emotion and constraints rather than analysis. This is the only *durable* edge.

**How to apply.** Every thesis's *variant perception* must name: (a) the specific behavioral or
structural source of the mispricing; (b) **who is on the other side and why they're irrational or
forced**; (c) the catalyst and rough timing for the correction. No named source → it's an
informational claim → reject per P1. *Caveat:* irrationality can persist a long time — pair every
behavioral edge with a catalyst and the horizon/sizing to wait it out (→ P3, P4).

## P3 — Long-term only; hold the thesis, not the price
**Principle.** Minimum **1-year-plus** horizon. Evaluate every idea on a multi-year basis. We do
not trade on short-term price moves.

**Why.** (1) A long horizon is what *lets* you harvest behavioral mispricing — you can afford to be
early and wait for the correction. (2) Lower costs, lower taxes, less noise, fewer decisions = fewer
mistakes.

**How to apply.** Reject any thesis whose payoff needs precise timing or a sub-1-year hold.
"Don't trade" ≠ "never sell": **hold for the thesis, exit on the thesis** — sell when a kill
criterion fires, the target/valuation is reached, or for disciplined rebalancing; never on noise.

## P4 — Balanced and hedged, but not timid
**Principle.** Build a portfolio that hedges risk yet keeps meaningful growth exposure —
diversified and resilient, *not* conservative.

**Why.** Idea-picking (P1–P3) is separate from survival. A great idea sized wrong, or a portfolio
that forces selling at the bottom, destroys returns. You must survive being wrong/early to collect
the long-term win.

**How to apply.**
- **No single thesis can cause permanent impairment** — size so the worst case is a dent. Separate
  *allocation bets* (worst case: poor returns) from *solvency bets* (worst case: zero) and size
  accordingly.
- **Diversify across uncorrelated exposures/regimes** — don't be concentrated in one crowded trade.
- **Keep ballast + dry powder** — defensives/hedges and optionality to *buy the irrational dips*
  (this is how you act on P2).
- **But stay growth-tilted** — over-conservatism (all cash/bonds) loses to inflation and forfeits
  compounding. "Hedged, not timid."

## P5 — Protect the compounding (the actual goal)
**Principle.** Long-term wealth comes from **compounding** — returns earning returns over time. The
job is to keep the compounding chain unbroken and **let winners run**. Compounding wins.

**Why.** Time is the dominant variable: a few uninterrupted compounders dwarf many small realized
wins. Two things kill compounding — (1) **interrupting it** (selling winners early, trading, churn →
taxes and fees, which are compounding leaks); and (2) **catastrophic loss** (a −50% drawdown needs
+100% just to break even; ruin resets the chain to zero). This is *why* P3 (patience) and P4 (no
wipeouts) exist — they are in service of P5.

**How to apply.**
- **Favor durable compounders** — businesses/assets that can reinvest at high returns for years —
  and let working positions run; don't trim a valid thesis just to "take profits."
- **Only interrupt compounding for a thesis reason** (kill criterion, target reached, rebalance) — per P3.
- **Treat avoiding permanent loss as a compounding decision**, not merely a risk one — per P4.
- **Minimize friction** — churn, taxes, and fees each silently erode the compounding rate.
