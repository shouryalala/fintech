## Mechanisms

**Why an equity pop often does NOT drag housing down:**
1. **Weak equity wealth-effect.** Case-Quigley-Shiller / NBER w8606: housing wealth has a larger,
   more robust consumption effect than stock wealth; the stock-wealth→consumption link is weak/contested.
2. **Rate-cut offset dominates.** Standard response = aggressive easing → cheaper mortgages → can
   *boost* prices. 2001–03 is the textbook case (11 cuts, refi boom, prices up while stocks −49%).
3. **Flight to hard assets** — capital rotates from discredited equities into real assets.

**When it DOES drag housing down — required amplifiers:**
1. **Shared collateral/credit channel (Japan)** — banks lending against both markets fuse them;
   equity fall destroys lending capacity → land falls. Structural, not wealth effect.
2. **Concentrated local job/income shock (SF 2001, NYC/Boston 1987)** — where the bubble sector *is*
   the local economy, layoffs cut local demand; high-tier + rentals first; lagged 6–24mo.
3. **Debt-deflation / systemic collapse (1929)** — mass unemployment, not a wealth channel.

**Lag pattern:** housing is illiquid/sticky; Case-Shiller is a 3-mo moving average of repeat sales
(reporting lag). Observed: ~6mo (SF 2001), ~9mo (Japan land vs equities), years (1987 job-driven).
Housing almost never reacts instantly to an equity peak.

## Bottom line — "what history actually says"
**An equity-bubble pop does NOT reliably drag residential RE down. Base rate = resilience or even
strength**, because the stock-wealth→housing link is weak and the policy reflex (deep rate cuts) is
a direct tailwind. The cleanest modern analog — **dot-com — saw national home prices keep rising**;
weakness was *regional and job-driven* (SF high-tier −12% / Santa Clara −19%, recovering in 2–3yr).
1987 confirms it (−31% equities, ~no national housing effect). **Housing falls only with a
structural amplifier** (shared bank collateral, or bubble-sector = local economy). **2008 is the
reverse case, not a counterexample** — RE led and caused the equity crash.

## Data-thin / contested flags
- **SF Bay Case-Shiller excludes Santa Clara County (core Silicon Valley).** The "−12% Bay Area"
  (5-county Case-Shiller) and "−19% Santa Clara SFH" (separate median/avg series) are **different
  indices** — not the same number.
- **Case-Shiller lag** (3-mo MA, repeat-sales) → published peak/trough dates lag reality by months.
- **1929 Florida** figures are anecdotal; no reliable 1920s price index.
- **1987 regional declines** from a secondary blog, not a primary index; multi-year lag confounds with 1990–91 recession.
- **Wealth-effect magnitude genuinely contested** (Fed vs Case-Quigley-Shiller) — direction
  (housing > stocks) is consensus; size uncertain.
- **GFC national peak-to-trough (~−27%)** varies by index vintage.
