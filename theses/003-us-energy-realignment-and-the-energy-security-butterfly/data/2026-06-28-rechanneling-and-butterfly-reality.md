## Q2 — US export re-channeling: a swing backfill, not a structural winner
**Bull-side (verified):** Atlantic-Basin (incl. US) crude exports rose ~3.5 mbd after the Feb onset (IEA OMR May-26); US→Asia crude hit a record **2.634 mbd (May 2026)**; ~600 kbd of US crude scheduled for China (Apr 2026); US is already the largest LNG exporter; US crude output record **>13.6 mbd (2025)**.

**Counter-case (the stronger side):**
- 🔴 **Demand was destroyed, not cleanly substituted:** Chinese gas imports **−11% y/y**, seaborne LNG **−22%**, crude **−2.8%** (Mar 2026) — no evidence the US filled the gap.
- 🔴 **China is structurally cushioned & largely bypasses the pivot:** >¼ of demand domestic, ~1.2 Bn-bbl onshore stockpile, Russia/Central Asia pipeline substitution.
- 🔴 **The US edge mean-reverts on reopening:** by **25 Jun 2026** WTI-Midland's Asia diff flipped from premium to a **~45¢ discount** as ADNOC/Kuwait/Iraq ramped spot — the surge looks temporary.
- 🔴 **US infrastructure is the binding limit:** ~15 Bcf/d operating + ~17 under construction + ~19 permitted-but-not-started (hesitation despite permits) ✓3-0 (CRS R48038); terminals cost $10–20B, multi-year; next wave (Golden Pass, CC Stage 3, Port Arthur, Rio Grande) lands in volume **2027+**.
- 🟡 **Allied offtake is real but pre-crisis:** JERA's **20-yr, up-to-5.5-Mtpa** US deals (Jun 2025) ✓3-0 — but signed *before* the crisis (weak Hormuz attribution); Europe still reluctant on 15–20yr deals.

🔴 **Read (kill criterion #2 partially fires):** US is a **swing backfill that mean-reverts when Gulf flows reopen**; the durable claimant of displaced Gulf demand is **Asian re-sourcing, not US exporters**. This leg is the **weakest / closest to already-priced** on a *structural* (vs opportunistic) basis. Note: US LNG still grows structurally on its own merits — just not *because of* Hormuz.

## Q4 — The butterfly: nuclear capex is real; the *security-shock causation* is weak
**Nuclear — real capex (verified):** ~**63 reactors / >70 GW under construction worldwide** (most since 1990); annual nuclear investment **+~50% since 2020 to >$60B** ✓3-0 (IEA). Uranium demand structurally rising (+28% by 2030); mine supply historically meets only **74–90%** of needs.
- 🟢 **Under-appreciated angle:** the **fuel-cycle/HALEU bottleneck** — enrichment **>99% concentrated in 4 suppliers, Russia ~40%** ✓3-0 (IEA) — is a genuine, policy-funded ($2.7B) security play.
- 🔴 **SMRs are narrative for this horizon:** first commercial units ~2030; operating SMRs ran **300–400% cost overruns**; IEEFA: "too expensive, too slow, too risky" for 10–15 yrs.
- ⚠️ **China — not the US/allies — dominates the buildout** (~half of reactors under construction).

**Solar — the weak, policy-headwinded leg (verified):** solar = 72% of new US capacity (Jan–Oct 2025) but only ~12% of installed base; deploys in ~1yr. BUT the driver is **data-center demand, not security**, and the **One Big Beautiful Bill Act curtails IRA credits** (wind/solar must start construction by **4 Jul 2026**; residential credit ends 2025) → **US solar industry expected to *contract* in 2026.**

⚠️ **The causation caveat that matters most:** IEA attributes the nuclear/solar revival to **energy security AND climate AND AI/data-center demand simultaneously** — and the **dominant *identifiable* driver is AI/data-center demand-pull, not the Hormuz security shock.** The "butterfly from the chokepoint" is the **softest claim in the whole thesis.**

**Read:** Nuclear-as-capex is real and the fuel-cycle/restart angle is under-appreciated; **but the butterfly is mostly an AI-power story wearing an energy-security jacket** — SMRs are narrative, and solar is demand-pulled into a 2026 policy contraction. Attribution to Hormuz specifically is weak (brushes kill criterion #4's spirit).
